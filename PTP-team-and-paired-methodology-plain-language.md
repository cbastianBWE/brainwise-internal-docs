# How the BrainWise Team and Paired Reports Work
### A plain-language guide, with the BrainWise labels named

This explains how we turn individual Personal Threat Profile results into a team report and into a two-person (paired) report. It covers what we did, why we did it, and what it means in practice. The actual labels we use are shown in **bold** the first time each appears, and gathered in a quick-reference glossary after Part 1.

---

## The starting point: what the PTP measures

The PTP looks at how a person's brain scans for two things under pressure: what feels like a threat, and what feels like a reward. It measures this across five areas, the **5P dimensions**. Three are about threat: **Protection** (feeling safe), **Participation** (feeling you belong), and **Prediction** (feeling you understand what is coming). Two are about reward: **Purpose** (meaning) and **Pleasure** (enjoyment).

Two ideas carry through everything below. First, the scores that matter most are the very high and very low ones, because those are the traits most likely to drive how someone reacts when stressed. Second, the brain only reaches for reward once it feels safe enough. A person, or a team, cannot settle into shared Purpose and Pleasure while the safety needs are still unmet.

---

## Part 1: The building blocks (how each trait is labeled)

A team or pair report has to interpret, not just display numbers. To do that automatically and consistently, we labeled every one of the 89 traits once, ahead of time. These labels are fixed properties of the instrument. They do not change from one team to the next. The team's actual scores get read against these labels each time.

Each trait carries two main labels.

**Label one is the Resource logic. It answers: what does it mean when a whole group scores high on this?** There are four possibilities, plus a neutral case.

- **Rivalrous.** It competes for something scarce. If everyone wants it, they collide. The need to be heard, the need for recognition, the need for control, the need for status.
- **Convergent.** It works best when shared. Agreement binds the group together, and disagreement causes friction. Trusting each other, valuing consistency, caring about fairness, sharing the same values.
- **Complementary.** Difference is an asset. You actually want a mix, and the mix becomes roles. Some people move fast, some think first. Some take risks, some check them.
- **Saturating.** Too much of it is the problem. Here, everyone scoring high is itself the dysfunction. If a whole team needs to conform, that is groupthink. If everyone fears looking foolish, nobody speaks up. If everyone needs to play devil's advocate, nothing gets decided.
- **Neutral.** It does not pull a group either way. Most personal and identity traits sit here, and the report does not route them to a signal.

**Label two is the Floor risk. It answers: what does it mean when a whole group scores low on this?** Two possibilities.

- **Protective.** It is a watch-out. Low means nobody is keeping an eye on a particular risk: awareness of what is going on around them, caution about money, attention to health and wellbeing. When a whole group is low here, that gauge is simply unwatched.
- **Neutral.** Low just means low, with no special group risk attached.

On top of those two labels, each trait carries three practical notes.

- **Dimension.** Which of the five areas (the 5P) it belongs to.
- **Group lean.** Whether it tends to pull a group toward **Dissension**, toward **Alignment**, or whether it is **Role-dependent** (or **Neutral**). This is a quick read that follows from the Resource logic.
- **Behavioral salience.** Whether it is a **Loud** trait you can see in someone's behavior, or a **Quiet** one that lives inside them and is easy to miss (or **Moderate** in between). Loud traits override quiet ones in what other people actually observe.

Separately, we mapped which traits help each other and which fight each other, both inside one person and between two people. This is the **Facet interactions** layer, and it uses four relationship labels: **Complements** (they reinforce), **Amplifies** (one intensifies the other, sometimes into a risk), **Detracts** (they pull against each other), and **Masks** (a loud trait hides a quiet one in visible behavior). Each pairing is tagged **within-person** or **cross-person**.

**Why we do this once.** Making these calls a single time, and writing them down, means every future report is generated the same way. The hard human judgment happens deliberately and is owned as part of the method, rather than being improvised differently for each client. The numbers are raw material. The labels are what let the report say something true.

## Quick reference: the labels

**Resource logic** (what it means when a group scores high)
- **Rivalrous** — competes for something scarce; everyone high collides. Routes to Collision.
- **Convergent** — works best when shared; agreement binds, disagreement rubs. Routes to Alignment when shared, Friction when split.
- **Complementary** — difference is useful and becomes roles. Routes to Complementarity.
- **Saturating** — too much is the problem; everyone high is the dysfunction. Routes to Groupthink, Rigidity, Silence, or Gridlock.
- **Neutral** — does not drive group dynamics. Not routed.

**Floor risk** (what it means when a group scores low)
- **Protective** — a watch-out; low means an unwatched risk. Routes to Blind spot.
- **Neutral** — low just means low.

**Group lean** — Dissension, Alignment, Role-dependent, or Neutral.

**Behavioral salience** — Loud (drives visible behavior, overrides others), Moderate, or Quiet (internal, easily masked).

**Dimension (the 5P)** — Protection, Participation, Prediction (the three threat dimensions), Purpose, Pleasure (the two reward dimensions).

**Used in** — Team + Paired (the 47 work-context traits) or Paired only (the 42 personal traits).

**Team signals** — Friction, Collision, Blind spot, Complementarity, Alignment, plus the Saturating outcomes (Groupthink, Rigidity, Silence, Gridlock).

**Shape** — tight cluster, gradient, or two camps (also called bimodal), found with the gap rule.

**Interactions** — Complements, Amplifies, Detracts, Masks, each tagged within-person or cross-person.

## Part 2: The team report

### What it is
It takes the individual results of everyone on a team and produces one picture of how the team, as a group, handles pressure and change. It uses the 47 work-context traits, the ones marked **Team + Paired**.

### The core idea
A team's risk lives in two places, not one. The obvious one is where people clash. The hidden one is where they quietly agree. A report that only highlighted differences would miss half the story, because some of the most serious patterns are forms of agreement, not disagreement.

### The signals it produces
Reading the labels against the team's actual scores, every trait routes into one of these.

- **Friction.** A **Convergent** trait where the team is split. People are running on different settings on something the group needs to agree on, and they rub against each other.
- **Collision.** A **Rivalrous** trait where almost everyone is high. This looks like cohesion because everyone is similar, but it is competition for the same scarce thing, like airtime or recognition or control.
- **Blind spot.** A **Protective** trait where almost everyone is low. Nobody is naturally tracking that risk, so it tends to arrive unannounced.
- **Complementarity.** A **Complementary** trait where the team differs. Left alone it reads as friction. Assigned as roles, it becomes a strength.
- **Alignment.** A **Convergent** trait where the team is tightly clustered at a healthy level. A genuine, usable strength.
- The **Saturating** outcomes (**Groupthink, Rigidity, Silence, Gridlock**). A Saturating trait where almost everyone is high. The uniformity itself is the warning.

The same spread of scores can mean Friction or Complementarity. What tells them apart is the trait's Resource logic label, which is why the labeling work matters.

### The shape check (why an average can lie)
Knowing the team's average and how spread out it is still is not enough, because a single spread number cannot tell three very different situations apart: a **tight cluster** near the middle, an even **gradient** across the range, or **two camps** split into opposed groups. Two camps is the real fault line, and it is dangerous precisely because it averages out to a calm-looking middle score. So the report runs the **gap rule**: it lines up everyone's scores in order and looks for a clear gap that breaks the team into two groups. If it finds one, it flags two camps and names the size and center of each. There is also a guard built in, so any two-camp trait is pulled out of **Alignment** no matter how moderate its average looks.

### The reading order: safety first
The report follows the PTP's own principle at the group level. It reads the team's threat picture first (**Protection, Participation, Prediction**), then asks whether the conditions even exist for the team to reach shared **Purpose** and **Pleasure**.

### What a coach does with each signal
- For **Friction**, build a shared agreement on the one or two traits where the team is most split.
- For **Collision**, change the structure rather than the people. Build turn-taking into meetings, or distribute recognition on a schedule so it stops being a prize.
- For **Blind spot**, assign the missing function to a named person, because by definition nobody will pick it up on their own.
- For **Complementarity**, turn the difference into a division of labor.
- For the **Saturating** outcomes, the move is usually to deliberately invite the missing voice: dissent, change, or candor.

## Part 3: The paired report

### What it is
It takes two people and produces one report about the relationship between them, built for pairs like a manager and a direct report, two co-founders, or two close peers. It uses all 89 traits, both **Team + Paired** and **Paired only**, because a relationship spans work and personal life.

### The core idea
For two people, the read is simple and clean. On every trait there is a distance between them, plus a question of whether they are both high, both low, or far apart. The interpretation comes from reading those three positions against the trait's labels.

- **Both high.** Usually a shared strength. But if the trait is **Rivalrous**, both being high is a **Collision** waiting to happen.
- **Both low.** A shared **Blind spot**, especially on a **Protective** trait.
- **Far apart.** Either useful **Complementarity** or **Friction**, depending on the trait. The label tells you which.

### The part that matters most here: how the two people combine
This is where the **Facet interactions** layer earns its keep.

First, how traits combine inside each person. Someone with a high need to be heard sitting next to low self-worth will show up confident and vocal while privately feeling fragile. The report surfaces these internal tensions, the **Detracts** pairings, so the partner understands what they are actually dealing with.

Second, **Behavioral salience**. When a **Loud** trait is high and a **Quiet** trait is low in the same person, the loud one dominates what the other person sees and **Masks** the quiet one. The report points out where one person is likely reading only the visible half of the other.

### The signature output
The most useful thing the paired report produces is two plain statements: what the first person needs from the second in order to feel safe and work well, and what the second needs from the first. It pairs that with a stress pattern: under pressure, one person tends to do this, the other tends to do that, and here is exactly where the two reactions collide, plus the move that defuses it.

### What a coach does with it
Use the two needs statements as the spine of a conversation between the pair. Name the shared blind spots out loud so they stop falling through the gap. Where the two are far apart on a **Complementary** trait, frame it as a division of labor. Where they collide on a **Rivalrous** trait, set an explicit agreement about how they will share it.

## A note on the numbers (the thresholds)

The report needs cut points to decide what counts as high, low, tightly clustered, or far apart. The current set: **high** means a score of 67 or above, **low** means 33 or below, a **tight cluster** means everyone falls within a 25-point range, and **two camps** means a gap of at least 25 points with a meaningful group on each side. For two people, **far apart** means a distance of 40 or more. These are sensible defaults and should be tuned to line up with how the individual report already labels people, so all three reports tell a consistent story.

## Judgment calls and rationale

Two markers sit behind the labels. They are worth understanding, because they are what keep the method both honest about its own limits and open to correction.

**Some labels are judgment calls, and they are flagged for review.** Most traits sorted cleanly into their labels. A handful did not, and rather than bury that, those are flagged so they can be checked and changed. There are three kinds. A few traits carry two meanings at once instead of one, such as the need to be right, which both competes (everyone needing to be right collides) and protects (a team with none of it gets sloppy). Two traits run backwards from the rest, where a low score, not a high one, is the warning sign, so they are flagged so the report does not misread them. And a few traits are healthy in moderation but tip into rigidity when everyone is high, so they carry a note about that ceiling. The purpose of these flags is honesty. They mark exactly where a person made an interpretive call that someone else could reasonably make differently.

**Every trait carries a written rationale.** Next to each trait is a short sentence explaining why it received the labels it did. Physical safety, for example, reads as a safety gauge that goes unwatched when a whole team scores low on it. Financial caution reads as a double case: pair the cautious with the risk-takers, but a team with none of it has an unwatched downside. This exists so the classification can be defended and corrected. You, a fellow facilitator, or a client's HR team can read the reasoning behind any label and challenge the logic, not just the conclusion. It is the difference between a black box and a method you can stand behind.

---

## What stays human

The report automates the interpretation, but two things remain human judgment. The labels themselves were assigned deliberately and can be revisited. And the **Facet interactions** map is a curated starting set, not the last word; it grows as coaches use the reports and notice patterns worth adding. The report is meant to open a conversation, not close one.
