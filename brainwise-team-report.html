AI interpretation

Team profile overview

This team reads the world primarily through Prediction (average 61), with Protection close behind (58) and Participation the softest and most uneven of the three (44). In plain terms, the group is wired to want clarity, influence, and a say in how decisions get made. It is reasonably alert to fairness and risk. It is least settled on belonging and trust, which is also where its members differ most. Following the 5P principle, the team will not reach a shared sense of purpose and momentum until that Participation floor steadies.

The pattern worth watching is not only where the team disagrees, it is where it quietly agrees. The need to be heard, to be recognized, and to be right all sit high with little variation, which turns airtime, credit, and being correct into scarce resources the group competes for rather than shares. At the same time the team is uniformly low on tracking what is going on around it and on near-term downside, so those risks are unwatched by default. Where the team genuinely splits, on pace and on appetite for change, the split is clean enough to be turned into roles rather than left as friction.

AI interpretation

What it means for the team

  • The team's biggest risk is collision, not apathy. A room of people who all need voice, credit, and to be right will compete for the same scarce things unless the structure shares them out.
  • The team is most exposed where it is silently unanimous. Nobody is naturally watching the surroundings or the near-term downside, so those tend to arrive as surprises.
  • The team's real differences in pace and appetite for change are an asset in waiting. Named and assigned, they become a division of labor instead of recurring friction.

What we found, and what to do

Four patterns showed up in this team. Each card is a simple picture of how the group sits, what it means, and the one move that addresses it.

Collision

Almost everyone scores high on the same scarce thing. Here it is the need to be heard. All twelve are crowded at the top, reaching for the same floor.

Do this: share it on a schedule. Turn-taking and rotating who runs the meeting, so airtime stops being a contest.

Friction

The team is split into two camps that pull against each other. Here it is how consistent to be. Half want tight standards, half want flexibility.

Do this: name the split out loud and agree one standard together, so it stops being re-fought on every decision.

Complementarity

The team is split here too, but the difference is a strength. Here it is pace. One group moves fast, the other thinks first. Together they cover both.

Do this: give each group the work it is built for. Movers drive execution, thinkers handle planning and review.

Blind spot

Almost everyone scores low, so no one is watching this. Here it is awareness of what is changing around the team. The risk arrives unseen.

Do this: assign it to one person on purpose. No one will pick it up on their own.

Saturating

Almost everyone scores high on the same thing, and here that is the problem. On the fear of looking foolish, when everyone is high no one risks speaking up. It looks like harmony, but it is silence.

Do this: make it safe to be wrong. Invite dissent by name and reward whoever raises the awkward point.

Alignment

Here the team is tightly clustered at a healthy level. Everyone trusts each other and values fair treatment. This is genuine common ground, the strength to build on.

Do this: name it and lean on it. Use this shared ground to carry the harder conversations above.

AI interpretation

Three suggested next steps

1

Build an airtime and credit architecture

Addresses collision
  • Use a written round before open discussion on real decisions, so the highest-voice members do not set the frame first.
  • Rotate facilitation rather than defaulting to the loudest or most senior person.
  • Distribute recognition on a standing cadence, naming specific contributions, so credit stops being a scarce prize.
  • Separate being heard from being decided: capture dissent in writing, then commit, so people feel counted even when the call goes another way.
2

Assign the unwatched gauges

Addresses blind spots
  • Name an owner for situational awareness, responsible for surfacing what is changing in the market, the client, and the org each cycle.
  • Require a one-line risk-and-cost note on any decision above a set threshold.
  • Add a standing "what are we not seeing" prompt to retrospectives, since the team will not raise it on its own.
  • Put one external check on the blind-spot areas rather than relying on the team to catch what it is wired to miss.
3

Turn the pace-and-change split into roles

Friction into complementarity
  • Map each member on pace (move fast versus think first) and appetite for change, and make the map visible.
  • Route execution-heavy work to the movers, and planning, sequencing, and review to the deliberators.
  • Agree an explicit rule for when the team moves fast and when it slows, so tempo stops being relitigated each time.
  • Pair opposite types on high-stakes work so the difference operates as a built-in check.

AI interpretation

How this team communicates

In general

This team is verbal, opinionated, and standards-driven. With voice, recognition, and the need to be right all high and shared, conversation tends to be a contest of strong views, and the most comfortable speakers set the frame quickly. Because the team is low on tracking context, members often arrive with different unspoken assumptions and do not realize it. They communicate best when an explicit standard or framework is on the table to organize around.

Under pressure

The loud needs get louder. The drive to be heard and to be right crowds the channel, quieter members and quieter traits like self-doubt go underground, and the team can mistake the most confident voice for the most correct one. Decisions start to feel made before everyone has spoken.

How to avoid communication conflict
  • Put a written round before open discussion, so the loudest voice does not set the frame for everyone else.
  • Anchor debates to an explicit criterion or goal, which this team aligns on, rather than to who is most insistent.

AI interpretation

How this team handles conflict

This team's conflict erupts along two clean lines: how consistent and reliable things should be, and how fast to move. On both, the team is split into two camps rather than scattered, which makes the disagreement feel like a faction line rather than ordinary friction. Combined with the high need to be right, conflict can become a contest to win rather than a problem to solve, and because the team is uneven on belonging and trust, those contests can leave some members feeling on the outside.

Mitigate it

Name the two fault lines out loud and build a standing agreement on each, so the team stops relitigating tempo and standards on every decision. Depersonalize disputes by routing them through the shared evaluation criteria the team already values, so the argument is about the standard, not the person.

Promote healthy conflict

The hidden risk is the opposite of too much heat. If the need to avoid looking foolish or to conform runs high, the team goes quiet rather than surface real disagreement, which looks like harmony but is avoidance. Invite dissent explicitly, rotate a devil's advocate, and reward whoever raises the uncomfortable point, so disagreement attaches to ideas and not to standing.
